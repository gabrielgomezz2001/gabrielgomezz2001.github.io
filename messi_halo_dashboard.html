So did Messi actually lift MLS — or just Inter Miami?

Everyone keeps saying he transformed the whole league's commercial fortunes. I went looking for the receipts. The story turned out more interesting than the headlines.

First, the headline numbers

The "Messi lifted MLS" story isn't wrong. It's just incomplete.

MLS sponsorship money grew from $461M to $665M in two years. That's a real, big jump. But the question isn't whether the league grew — it's where the growth came from. That's where the simple story falls apart.

Wait — what's a "halo"?

Quick detour for anyone new to this.

01

Think Tiger Woods.

When Tiger was at his peak, every PGA event got more eyeballs and more sponsor interest — even tournaments he wasn't playing in. His being in the sport made the whole sport more valuable. That ripple effect is what people call a "halo."

02

The Messi version of that idea.

The story everyone tells: Messi made MLS culturally interesting in a way it had never been. Brands that ignored MLS started paying attention. Even teams that never played Inter Miami got a boost — because being in MLS now meant being in the same league as Messi.

03

So "halo" = the spillover.

It's not Inter Miami's own growth. That's just Inter Miami winning. The halo is whether the other 28 teams are now in a better spot to sell sponsorships than they were before Messi showed up.

04

Why this matters for everyone else.

If you run a non-Miami team, you don't care that Inter Miami got rich. You care whether your sponsorship sales are happening in a better market because of him. So: did Messi make MLS a better place to sell, or did he just make one team better off?

Quick spoiler: the answer turns out to be "he just made one team better off." But the proof needs a few different angles to be airtight. Here's the math.

Three angles, one answer

This isn't just one chart. It's a pattern.

If a halo were really there, you'd see it from multiple angles — in the overall numbers, in which categories grew, and in which teams gained the most. Instead, every angle says the same thing.

The other 28 teams grew 34%. The trend already predicted 33%.

Before Messi was even a rumor, MLS sponsorship was growing 15% a year. That's not a small number — it's healthy growth driven by league expansion, the 2026 World Cup approaching, and the new Apple TV deal.

If you take that 15%/year rate and run it for two years, you get ~33% growth. That's the "do nothing different, no Messi" prediction.

What actually happened to the other 28 teams? They grew 34%.

That extra 1 point — about $5M total spread across 28 teams — is everything we can blame Messi for, outside Miami. It's less than a single decent sleeve patch deal. Calling that a "halo" is a stretch.

"Once you take out the trend that was already there, there's basically nothing left for Messi to take credit for, outside Miami."
If your team's sponsor revenue grew 30-35% in this window, you didn't catch a Messi halo — you caught the same growth MLS was already producing without him. To actually beat the trend, you needed to grow 40%+. Most teams didn't.

Three categories drove the 2024 jump. Two have nothing to do with Messi.

SponsorUnited points to three categories that made up over $50M of MLS's 2023→2024 growth: Hotel/Restaurant/Leisure, Business Services, and Alcohol. The Messi story would say: this is where the halo lives. Let me check each one.

Hotel/Restaurant/Leisure: SponsorUnited's CEO said directly that this surge was driven mostly by one deal — Royal Caribbean's jersey sponsorship with Inter Miami (~$8-10M/year). That's not a halo. That's Miami again.

Business Services: Real, broad growth here. The standout was Chicago Fire tripling their business services deals — driven by a Chicago specialty steel company expanding its commitment. That's a sales win, not Messi spillover.

Alcohol: Up across NFL, NBA, MLS, and F1 at the same time. A global trend in sports as drinking habits shift toward spirits, ready-to-drinks, and seltzers. Has nothing to do with one MLS player.

"Even the categories supposedly 'unlocked by Messi' turn out to be mostly other stories wearing his hat."
If you're trying to ride the Messi wave by chasing the categories the press credits him for, two of the three are actually being driven by things you can already pitch — local B2B and global drink trends. No global superstar required.

Chicago Fire jumped from 16th to 2nd in deals signed. They did it without using Messi's name once.

Of every team in MLS, Chicago Fire had the biggest one-year jump in deal count over this window — climbing 14 spots in one year. If a halo would show up clearly anywhere, it should show up here.

But what's actually happening here? Four specific moves — and any MLS team could run them.

Move 01
Land a national brand on your best jersey spot.

Carvana isn't from Chicago — they're a national online used-car company. The front of the jersey is the most valuable spot any soccer club has, usually a $5-15M/year multi-year deal. Chicago used their best inventory to land a big national advertiser. That's the upgrade move every team is trying to make.

Big national account
Move 02
Sell more to partners you already have.

Hyundai was already a Chicago Fire partner. Instead of leaving that as is, Chicago sold them the sleeve patch — a separate paid spot on the other part of the jersey. This is the easiest sale a team can make: take a partner who already trusts you, find more inventory to sell them, grow the deal. Sales people call it "expansion revenue" and it's the highest-ROI work they do.

Grow existing partner
Move 03
Win back old partners with a small deal first.

Magellan, a Chicago specialty steel company, used to sponsor the Fire but had stopped. Chicago brought them back in December 2023 as an "Official Partner" — a small, low-tier deal mostly about events and presence. Five months later, they upgraded Magellan to a jersey sleeve deal — much bigger money. Classic relationship sales: a small re-entry deal as the door-opener for a big deal later.

Small first, then big
Move 04
Sell to your actual local market, not the league trend.

A used-car company. A new-car company. A specialty steel company. Two of those three are Chicago industries — the city has a huge auto market and is historically a steel and manufacturing town. They didn't chase Royal Caribbean or some flashy global brand. They sold to companies with a real reason to want Chicago market exposure.

Lean into your city
"A used-car company. A new-car company. A Chicago specialty steel company. The team that 'caught the Messi era' the most actually did it the old-fashioned way."
None of these four moves needed Messi, soccer's global moment, or being a flashy team. They needed a sales team that knew their inventory, knew their city, and knew how to grow accounts. Every move is repeatable. This is the actionable lesson from the whole project — the team that won most in this window didn't ride a wave, they ran a disciplined playbook.
The bottom line

Sales execution still wins. Even in the Messi era.

Everyone says Messi lifted MLS sponsorship 44%. The data says he made Inter Miami rich, and the rest of the league grew at exactly the rate it was already growing. The teams that actually won bigger — like Chicago Fire — did it through classic sales work, not by riding the wave. For any team that's not Inter Miami, the playbook hasn't changed.

How I did the math

League totals come from SponsorUnited's annual MLS Marketing Partnerships Reports (2021 through 2024).

Inter Miami's specific sponsorship numbers are estimates. The club shares total revenue but not the sponsorship line item, so I used midpoints of public ranges.

The "halo" math is simple: actual growth in the rest of the league, minus what the pre-Messi growth rate (15%/yr) would have produced over two years.
