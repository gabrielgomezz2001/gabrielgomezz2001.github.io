Beyond Miami · No. 01

So did Messi actually lift MLS — or just Inter Miami?

Everyone keeps saying he transformed the whole league's commercial fortunes. I went looking for the receipts. The data tells two stories at once: the directly measurable halo on the other 28 teams is small, but the structural and leading-indicator halo is real and hasn't fully compounded yet.

An analysis by Gabriel Gomez

First, the headline numbers

The "Messi lifted MLS" story isn't wrong. It's just incomplete.

MLS sponsorship money grew from $461M to $665M in two years. That's a real, big jump. But the question isn't whether the league grew — it's where the growth came from. That's where the simple story falls apart.

Wait — what's a "halo"?

Quick detour for anyone new to this.

01

Think Tiger Woods.

When Tiger was at his peak, every PGA event got more eyeballs and more sponsor interest — even tournaments he wasn't playing in. His being in the sport made the whole sport more valuable. That ripple effect is what people call a "halo."

02

The Messi version of that idea.

The story everyone tells: Messi made MLS culturally interesting in a way it had never been. Brands that ignored MLS started paying attention. Even teams that never played Inter Miami got a boost — because being in MLS now meant being in the same league as Messi.

03

So "halo" = the spillover.

It's not Inter Miami's own growth. That's just Inter Miami winning. The halo is whether the other 28 teams are now in a better spot to sell sponsorships than they were before Messi showed up.

04

Why this matters for everyone else.

If you run a non-Miami team, you don't care that Inter Miami got rich. You care whether your sponsorship sales are happening in a better market because of him. So: did Messi make MLS a better place to sell, or did he just make one team better off?

With that out of the way, the question becomes whether the rest of MLS actually saw any real lift, or whether the headline number is just Inter Miami doing the heavy lifting and the other teams growing the way they were already going to grow. Before I get into the dollar math though, a quick word on what the data can and can't tell us.
Before we dig in

What this analysis can and can't measure.

Quick honesty check before the numbers. The piece pulls from two different windows depending on what's being measured. Worth flagging upfront so the framing is clear.

01

The dollar math: 2022 to 2024.

That's the cleanest before and after I could pull from SponsorUnited's public reports, which only run through 2024. So when the piece compares total sponsorship dollars or growth rates, that's the window. It catches the immediate dollar response. It misses the slower stuff that doesn't land in a sponsorship total until year three, four, or five.

02

The structural story: 2022 to 2026.

For the bigger picture (new commercial categories opening up, player movement, the inventory ceiling moving) the window stretches into 2026. Lowe's, Nu Stadium, back of jersey, the Müller signing. Those happened after the SponsorUnited data closed but they're central to what the piece is actually arguing.

03

29 of 30 MLS clubs.

MLS now has 30 teams. San Diego FC didn't join until 2025, so they don't show up in the 2022 to 2024 sponsorship reports. Everything in here is based on the 29 clubs that existed during the comparison window. The other 28 teams, when I use that phrase, means 29 minus Inter Miami.

04

So we'll cover both layers.

First, the dollar math, where the simple story falls apart. Then the leading indicators and structural shifts that aren't in the totals yet. Both are real. Both matter.

Three angles, one answer

This isn't just one chart. It's a pattern.

If a halo were really there, you'd see it from multiple angles — in the overall numbers, in which categories grew, and in which teams gained the most. Instead, every angle says the same thing.

The other 28 teams grew 34%. The trend already predicted 33%.

Before Messi was even a rumor, MLS sponsorship was growing 15% a year. That's not a small number — it's healthy growth driven by league expansion, the 2026 World Cup approaching, and the new Apple TV deal.

If you take that 15%/year rate and run it for two years, you get ~33% growth. That's the "do nothing different, no Messi" prediction.

What actually happened to the other 28 teams? They grew 34%.

That extra 1 point — about $5M total spread across 28 teams — is everything we can blame Messi for, outside Miami. It's less than a single decent sleeve patch deal. Calling that a "halo" is a stretch.

"Once you take out the trend that was already there, there's basically nothing left for Messi to take credit for, outside Miami."
If your team's sponsor revenue grew 30-35% in this window, you didn't catch a Messi halo — you caught the same growth MLS was already producing without him. To actually beat the trend, you needed to grow 40%+. Most teams didn't.

Three categories drove the 2024 jump. Two have nothing to do with Messi.

SponsorUnited points to three categories that made up over $50M of MLS's 2023→2024 growth: Hotel/Restaurant/Leisure, Business Services, and Alcohol. The Messi story would say: this is where the halo lives. Let me check each one.

Hotel/Restaurant/Leisure: SponsorUnited's CEO said directly that this surge was driven mostly by one deal — Royal Caribbean's jersey sponsorship with Inter Miami (~$8-10M/year). That's not a halo. That's Miami again.

Business Services: Real, broad growth here. The standout was Chicago Fire tripling their business services deals — driven by a Chicago specialty steel company expanding its commitment. That's a sales win, not Messi spillover.

Alcohol: Up across NFL, NBA, MLS, and F1 at the same time. A global trend in sports as drinking habits shift toward spirits, ready-to-drinks, and seltzers. Has nothing to do with one MLS player.

"Even the categories supposedly 'unlocked by Messi' turn out to be mostly other stories wearing his hat."
If you're trying to ride the Messi wave by chasing the categories the press credits him for, two of the three are actually being driven by things you can already pitch — local B2B and global drink trends. No global superstar required.

Chicago Fire jumped from 16th to 2nd in deals signed. They did it without using Messi's name once.

Of every team in MLS, Chicago Fire had the biggest one-year jump in deal count over this window — climbing 14 spots in one year. If a halo would show up clearly anywhere, it should show up here.

But what's actually happening here? Four specific moves — and any MLS team could run them.

Move 01
Land a national brand on your best jersey spot.

Carvana isn't from Chicago — they're a national online used-car company. The front of the jersey is the most valuable spot any soccer club has, usually a $5-15M/year multi-year deal. Chicago used their best inventory to land a big national advertiser. That's the upgrade move every team is trying to make.

Big national account
Move 02
Sell more to partners you already have.

Hyundai was already a Chicago Fire partner. Instead of leaving that as is, Chicago sold them the sleeve patch — a separate paid spot on the other part of the jersey. This is the easiest sale a team can make: take a partner who already trusts you, find more inventory to sell them, grow the deal. Sales people call it "expansion revenue" and it's the highest-ROI work they do.

Grow existing partner
Move 03
Win back old partners with a small deal first.

Magellan, a Chicago specialty steel company, used to sponsor the Fire but had stopped. Chicago brought them back in December 2023 as an "Official Partner" — a small, low-tier deal mostly about events and presence. Five months later, they upgraded Magellan to a jersey sleeve deal — much bigger money. Classic relationship sales: a small re-entry deal as the door-opener for a big deal later.

Small first, then big
Move 04
Sell to your actual local market, not the league trend.

A used-car company. A new-car company. A specialty steel company. Two of those three are Chicago industries — the city has a huge auto market and is historically a steel and manufacturing town. They didn't chase Royal Caribbean or some flashy global brand. They sold to companies with a real reason to want Chicago market exposure.

Lean into your city
"A used-car company. A new-car company. A Chicago specialty steel company. The team that 'caught the Messi era' the most actually did it the old-fashioned way."
None of these four moves needed Messi, soccer's global moment, or being a flashy team. They needed a sales team that knew their inventory, knew their city, and knew how to grow accounts. Every move is repeatable. This is the actionable lesson from the whole project — the team that won most in this window didn't ride a wave, they ran a disciplined playbook.
What the dollar count misses, Part 1

Inter Miami isn't just capturing dollars. They're moving the ceiling on what MLS commercial inventory can sell for.

Sponsorship totals are a lagging measure. The leading edge is whether the structural commercial ceiling of the league is moving up. Tap any of these to see the receipts.

01

Front of jersey: new MLS record.

Royal Caribbean became Inter Miami's front of jersey sponsor in December 2023. Eight figure annual deal. Most lucrative front of jersey deal in MLS history at the time of signing.

The detail: Royal Caribbean replaced XBTO, which had been the front of jersey sponsor since 2021. The new deal got done five months after Messi's debut. Sports Business Journal reported it as the richest front of jersey deal in the league. That benchmark now sits in the back of every front of jersey negotiation in MLS.
02

Sleeve: two MLS records in two years.

Typical MLS sleeve deals run $1M to $2M a season. Inter Miami broke that ceiling twice in three years. First with Fracht in 2023, then with Lowe's in 2026 at eight figures a year.

The detail: Fracht Group signed the sleeve in July 2023, right before Messi debuted, at what was then the richest sleeve deal in MLS. Lowe's replaced Fracht in January 2026 at eight figures annually. SBJ called Lowe's the richest and longest sleeve sponsorship agreement in MLS history. Same team broke its own league record inside three years. That's a moving ceiling.
03

Stadium naming: a first for JPMorgan Chase, then leveled up.

Chase Stadium opened in February 2024. It was JPMorgan Chase's first ever stadium naming rights deal in pro soccer. Two years later it was already superseded by Nu Stadium.

The detail: Inter Miami announced JPMorgan Chase as the naming partner for Chase Stadium in February 2024. Chase had never put its name on a soccer venue before. In March 2026, Inter Miami announced a new stadium naming deal with Nu (a digital bank with 131M customers entering the US) for Nu Stadium at Miami Freedom Park, opening April 2026. Two record stadium naming deals in two years. Both for the same club.
04

Back of jersey: brand new MLS category, filled within a month.

In February 2026, MLS announced back of jersey ad space as a new league wide inventory category starting after the 2026 All-Star Break. By March 2026, Inter Miami had already sold theirs.

The detail: Back of jersey is the fourth sponsorship zone on MLS kits and the second largest space after the front. Sportico reported the announcement on Feb 11, 2026. Nu's logo will appear on the back of Inter Miami's jersey starting August 2026 as part of the same Nu Stadium partnership. That makes Inter Miami the first team in MLS history to sell back of jersey inventory. They were ready before the rule was even live.
The pattern. Every time MLS commercial inventory has been tested or expanded since Messi arrived, Inter Miami has set the new record. Each new ceiling becomes the benchmark every other team will negotiate against in the years ahead. That's halo at the league's commercial ceiling, even if it hasn't compounded into team level sponsorship growth for the other 28 clubs yet.
SBJ said it directly. Inter Miami "has operated in a league of its own in terms of sponsorship value" since Messi's arrival in July 2023. That's not a Miami press release. That's the trade publication that covers every commercial deal in American sports.
What the dollar count misses, Part 2

The other halo signal is who started showing up after Messi signed.

Sponsorship totals are the lagging measure. Player movement is the leading one. When top of game players start picking your league over retirement or other destinations, the commercial impact follows three to five years later. Tap any of these for the receipts.

01

Luis Suárez. Grêmio to Inter Miami. December 2023.

Won every major club trophy in Europe. Publicly cited the chance to reunite with Messi as a reason for choosing Miami. Designated Player, 36 years old at signing.

The detail: Suárez left Grêmio in Brazil specifically to reunite with the Barcelona front three of Messi and Jordi Alba in Miami. He was 36, still scoring at a top level. Inter Miami got him on a one year deal that turned into a multi year run. The signing happened five months after Messi's debut.
02

Olivier Giroud. AC Milan to LAFC. May 2024.

France's all time leading international scorer. World Cup winner in 2018. Free transfer, Designated Player. Said at his press conference that joining MLS was "destiny."

The detail: Giroud's contract had two years left at AC Milan with a Champions League run still alive. He chose to walk away and go to LAFC instead. Free transfer, no fee. Designated Player through 2025 with a 2026 option. His own quote at the introduction was that joining LAFC was "destiny" and that his dream had always been to play in MLS. Pre-Messi era, that's a quote you don't hear from a France international.
03

Marco Reus. Borussia Dortmund to LA Galaxy. August 2024.

Three time Bundesliga Player of the Season. Champions League finalist in his last Dortmund season. Told ESPN that America "was my first choice."

The detail: Reus spent 12 years at Dortmund and was a free agent after his contract expired in May 2024. He turned down European clubs and chose MLS. His full ESPN quote: "America, and especially the Galaxy, was my first choice." Two and a half year deal through end of 2026. He made his Galaxy debut on August 24, 2024, scoring and assisting to win Player of the Match.
04

Thomas Müller. Bayern Munich to Vancouver Whitecaps. August 2025.

2014 World Cup winner. 25 years at Bayern, 33 trophies, 250 goals. Largest signing in Vancouver Whitecaps history. Helped Vancouver reach the 2025 MLS Cup Final.

The detail: Bayern declined to renew Müller's contract after the 2024-25 season. He could have gone almost anywhere in world football. He chose the Vancouver Whitecaps. Free transfer. Largest signing in club history. Indigenous leaders greeted him at the airport with a crowd of fans. Nine goals and four assists in his first 13 games. Vancouver made the 2025 MLS Cup Final and lost to Inter Miami. Müller is back for 2026 on a Designated Player option.
The Ronaldo parallel. When Cristiano Ronaldo went to Saudi Arabia in December 2022, it opened the door for Benzema, Mané, Mahrez, and others to follow. Top players picking a non traditional league is itself a leading indicator. The same dynamic is now playing out with MLS. Four marquee European stars signed within 24 months of Messi's arrival. All in their mid thirties. All choosing MLS over retirement or other destinations. That's not a coincidence. That's agents telling their clients MLS is a real option in a way it wasn't pre Messi. The commercial value of that perception shift compounds slowly. It won't fully show up in sponsorship totals until 2027 or beyond.
It's not all Inter Miami

While Miami dominates the headlines, other MLS teams are quietly running their own playbook.

If you only read the press releases, you'd think Miami was the only MLS club doing real commercial work. That's not the picture. Here's where other teams are creating value the dollar count doesn't always capture. Tap any of these for more.

01

Chicago Fire. The execution story.

The team that grew sponsor count the most in this window. Climbed 14 spots in deal count without using Messi's name. Pure sales work.

The detail: Chicago Fire didn't ride a global wave. They signed four marquee deals in a year and a half: Hyundai (used car category), Polestar (new car), Chicago Specialty Steel, and a stadium naming refresh. Each one was a sales rep finding inventory and a buyer and matching them up. This is what's repeatable in every market. None of it needed a global superstar. That's the actionable lesson of the whole project.
02

LAFC. The most balanced front office in MLS.

Won the 2022 MLS Cup. Signed Giroud in 2024 without breaking the bank. Sells out BMO Stadium. Co-owners include Will Ferrell, Mia Hamm, Magic Johnson.

The detail: LAFC has been one of the league's most consistent commercial performers since launch in 2018. Founding sponsor portfolio reads like a Fortune 500 list. Adidas, BMW, Heineken USA. Stadium is sold out almost every match. They didn't need a transformative signing to compete commercially. They built the front office that way from day one. Giroud joining in 2024 was a luxury, not a rescue.
03

Atlanta United. The supporter culture is the product.

Highest average attendance in MLS for most of the post 2017 era. Built a brand identity around the Atlanta market and the Black and Gold supporter base.

The detail: Atlanta United has averaged 44,000+ fans per match for most of their existence. They built brand equity around the city and the supporter culture, not a single player. That foundation is why their sponsorship inventory has been valued at the top of the league for years. Their model is more sustainable than the Inter Miami model because it doesn't depend on one signing staying healthy and on the field.
04

The whole league. A new sponsorship category is opening up.

SponsorUnited's 2026 trend report calls out the rise of "business backed sponsorships." Enterprise brands like AWS, Cisco, Microsoft, and Sony are embedding into league operations as infrastructure, not marketing.

The detail: SponsorUnited's March 2026 insight on business backed sponsorships shows enterprise brands shifting how they value sports deals. Cisco runs NFL stadium networking. AWS powers NFL Next Gen Stats and NBA cloud infrastructure. Sony reshapes NHL broadcast tech. Microsoft Copilot is on every NFL sideline. The framing matters: sponsorship is moving from a marketing line item to an infrastructure budget. MLS hasn't fully tapped this category yet, which means there's whitespace for the team or league office that figures it out first. Whoever lands the first AWS x MLS or Cisco x MLS deal is going to set a new category benchmark, the same way Inter Miami did with the jersey deals.
The takeaway. Inter Miami's records make the news. They should. But the teams that are going to win the next decade aren't necessarily the ones with the loudest star. They're the ones with a sales team that knows their inventory, a brand identity that doesn't depend on one player, and a front office willing to chase new categories like business backed sponsorships before the rest of the league catches on.
The bottom line

Two stories, both true. The headline number was misleading. The halo is still real.

In the 2022 to 2024 sponsorship window, the directly measurable halo on the other 28 MLS teams was small. Inter Miami captured the lion's share of new dollars and the rest of the league grew roughly at the rate it was already growing. The teams that outperformed, like Chicago Fire, did it through classic sales execution. But that window only captures the lagging dollar layer. The leading indicators tell a different story. Top European players choosing MLS. New commercial inventory categories opening up and being filled by Inter Miami. Agents and broadcasters treating the league as a real destination. All point to a structural halo that hasn't compounded yet. Real test of the framework: revisit this analysis in 2027.

How I did the math
