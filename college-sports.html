The Cost of Saving College Sports · No. 01

The federal government is now trying to save college sports from itself.

Universities are cutting academic programs. Olympic sports are getting dropped. Boosters are walking away. The President had to write two executive orders. The free market created a system nobody can fix.

Programs cut415+
Executive orders2
SourceHouse settlement filings, White House, ESPN, CBS Sports, SBJ
The cleanest example of what we built

Duke paid $8 million for Darian Mensah. They got him for one year.

In December 2024, Duke signed Darian Mensah to a two year, $8 million NIL deal. He was their guy. Above market rate sure, but he delivered.

3,973 passing yards 34 touchdowns Second team All-ACC First ACC title since 1962

After the season he publicly announced he was coming back for year two. Two months later he was at Miami for $10 million.

Duke sued. They lost. Mensah is now at a conference rival, and Duke and Miami play each other in 2026.

Quick detour · The terms you'll see in this piece

What "NIL" actually means, in 60 seconds.

If you're new to this space, three terms keep showing up. Here's what each one is, fast.

01

NIL · Name, Image, and Likeness.

Started July 2021 after a Supreme Court ruling. Athletes can now get paid for endorsements, social media, appearances, and merchandise. Before this, the NCAA banned it.

02

NIL Collective · The booster fund.

A pool of donor money that pays athletes to come to (or stay at) a specific school. Technically for "NIL services," functionally operates as recruiting money. The Aikman quote later in this piece is about a UCLA collective.

03

Revenue Sharing · Schools pay directly.

Brand new in July 2025. Schools can now pay athletes directly from school funds, capped at $20.5M per school per year. That cap grows to $32.9M by 2035.

04

Why all three matter at once.

Pre-2021, schools couldn't pay athletes. Today, athletes can get paid through all three channels at the same time. That's the system this piece is about.

To be clear · Before we go further

NIL is a good thing. I played D1. I'm not against it.

Athletes deserve to be paid. Full stop. Before we get into what's broken, here's what NIL actually got right.

01

Athletes finally getting paid for the value they create.

Football and basketball generate billions for their schools. The athletes generating that revenue used to get none of it. That was wrong. NIL fixed it.

02

Players from low-income backgrounds can support their families.

Before NIL, a future NBA pick from a poor family still had to wait years to help his mom pay rent. NIL changed that overnight. That's a real human win.

03

Players are staying in school longer.

NBA Draft early entries dropped from 363 in 2021 to 106 in 2025. Athletes are finishing their degrees AND earning real money. That's the system working.

04

A real labor market for the first time.

Coaches have always been free agents making millions. Now players have similar freedom. That's just basic fairness.

So this isn't an anti-NIL piece. The question isn't whether athletes should be paid. They should be. The question is whether the system around them is working, and right now it isn't. Let's get into why.
Section 01 · Universities are getting squeezed

The same schools paying NIL deals are cutting academic programs at a level we haven't seen before.

Here's what nobody is connecting. The universities writing seven figure NIL checks are also closing degree programs, laying off employees, and piling on debt. The two stories are actually one story.

Clemson · Athletics debt
$1.5B

Total athletics related debt. Cited in the April 2026 executive order as a national problem.

Syracuse · Programs closing
93

Academic programs being closed or paused. Announced April 2026.

UNC Chapel Hill · Cuts
$89M

Spending cuts over the next three years across the institution.

Duke · Layoffs
600

Employees laid off as part of a $350 million budget cut.

Indiana public colleges · Programs
580

Academic programs eliminated or merged statewide. Roughly a quarter of all academic offerings.

East Carolina · Program cuts
44

Programs cut alongside a $25 million reduction in spending.

This isn't a coincidence. Schools have to find revenue sharing money somewhere, and that money is coming from inside the building. The House v. NCAA settlement requires schools to share up to $20.5M per year with athletes starting July 2025, climbing to $32.9M by 2035.

The White House cited the numbers directly in the April 2026 executive order. One major athletic program closed fiscal year 2025 with $535 million in athletics related debt. Another has $437 million. Their own framing: "These financial perils will inevitably siphon funds from universities' educational and research purposes."

All while universities are losing students. Enrollment peaked at 21 million in 2010 and is now 19.6 million. The demographic cliff hits 2025. Fewer students AND higher athletic costs at the same moment. The math doesn't work.

Section 02 · Olympic sports are dying

The same squeeze is happening inside athletics. Just not to football or basketball.

More than 415 Olympic sports programs have been cut, merged, or eliminated since May 2024. The athletes finally getting paid are concentrated in two sports. The athletes getting cut are everywhere else.

01

Cleveland State Wrestling.

The team raised over $500,000 in donations and won a student led fee increase to stay alive. Didn't matter. The program got cut.

02

Cal Poly Swimming.

Cut both men's and women's swimming in one announcement. No replacement, no transition path for the athletes.

03

Grand Canyon Men's Volleyball.

Made the NCAA Final Four in 2024. Program cut shortly after. Going to the Final Four didn't save them.

04

Iowa State Gymnastics.

Suspended along with cuts at Hilton Coliseum. A program with decades of history, gone in a single announcement.

05

Georgia Tech Swimming and Diving.

Cut 19 swimmers and divers from the active roster. Scholarship slots gone overnight.

06

Tennis programs gone.

Eastern Illinois, Prairie View A&M, Radford, UTEP, Louisiana Monroe. Five programs eliminated in a single recruiting cycle.

The Olympic pipeline runs through these schools. America doesn't have a national sports academy system like most other countries. The universities ARE the pipeline. 75% of US Olympians in 2024 came through NCAA sports. Over 80% of US medalists at Paris 2024 competed in college first. Around 85% of the US wrestling team came through the college system. When these programs get cut, the pipeline gets cut.
From the inside · D1 Football, Towson

I played D1 football as a kicker at Towson. So I know how the locker room actually works. The QB and the skill position players are the ones getting the NIL deals, the same way the football and basketball stars are the ones getting paid in this whole system.

Some kickers and long snappers do well too, especially the ones with a real social media following. But most of the special teams guys, the walk on receivers, the backup linemen, they're not on the NIL boards. They're closer to the swimmers and wrestlers getting cut than they are to the QB making $4 million.

That perspective matters when you talk about who actually benefits from all of this. It's not "athletes" in general. It's a very specific slice of athletes.

Section 03 · The booster ROI is broken

The Mensah case isn't an exception. It's becoming the rule.

Boosters are funding a system where they have no leverage. Players don't know who's paying them. Donors don't get acknowledgment. Contracts can't be enforced. The player can leave after one year for more money somewhere else.

"I gave money to a kid. I won't mention who. I've done it one time at UCLA. Never met the young man. He was there a year, he left after the year. I wrote a sizable check, and he went to another school. I didn't even get so much as a thank you note. So, it's one of those deals to where I'm done with NIL."

Troy Aikman · UCLA alum · Three time Super Bowl champion

Aikman went on the Sports Media Podcast with Richard Deitsch and said he's done with NIL. The kicker: the player he funded didn't even know who paid him. According to a source familiar with the UCLA collective, it was standard practice for players not to know which donors contributed. The collective handled the routing.

So picture the system. Boosters write seven figure checks to athletes they've never met. Players don't know who funded them. Contracts can't be enforced. The player leaves after one year. The booster quits the system. That's not sustainable.

"If I write you a check for a million dollars, you can't stay for 300 days. You gotta at least give me 2 years."

Shaquille O'Neal · The Big Podcast with Shaq

"We're not recruiting any high school players."

Rick Pitino · St. John's basketball coach · February 2025

Pitino said the quiet part out loud. The transfer portal is so competitive that major programs aren't using roster spots on high schoolers anymore. They're grabbing proven players out of the portal. The next generation of athletes is getting locked out before they ever get to college.

The structural picture. Boosters write checks to players they've never met. Players don't know who's paying them. Contracts can't be enforced. Players leave after one year for more money. High school recruits get fewer scholarship slots. Boosters quit the system. That's not a funding model. That's a market that punishes the only people putting money into it.
Section 04 · A two conference monopoly (with outliers)

The boosters are walking away from most schools. But not from a few specific ones.

The talent is consolidating into two conferences plus a handful of well funded outliers. The gap between those programs and everyone else gets wider every year.

SEC + Big Ten · Retention
97.4%

Of eligible returning all-conference players in the SEC and Big Ten stayed at their school. The talent doesn't leave.

ACC + Big 12 · Retention
56.8%

The other two Power Four conferences kept just over half their all-conference talent. The rest left for the SEC or Big Ten.

Group of Six · Retention
29.5%

Less than a third of Group of Six all-conference players stayed. The rest are now feeding the Power Four.

Power Four overall · Retention
74.6%

The averaged Power Four retention hides the bigger story. The SEC and Big Ten are doing most of the keeping.

But the conference retention number isn't the whole picture. A few schools outside the SEC and Big Ten are competing at that level too. Not because of their conference. Because of one or two billionaire boosters with deep pockets.

01

Miami (ACC) · The John Ruiz era and Canes Connection.

Miami's 2025 NIL payroll hit around $30 million, putting them at the top of the ACC. Cam Ward signed for $1.5M, then $2.1M. Carson Beck signed for $4M+. The Mensah deal hit $10M. John Ruiz personally bankrolled over $20M in NIL deals to 100+ Miami athletes. Miami made the 2025 CFP National Championship game, their first in 25 years.

02

Texas Tech (Big 12) · The Matador Club and Cody Campbell.

Texas Tech's combined NIL and revenue sharing budget for 2025 is $55 million. The Matador Club, founded by billionaire booster Cody Campbell, has raised $63.3 million from 3,500 donors since 2022. JT Toppin ($2.8M), softball star NiJaree Canady (back to back million dollar deals). Texas Tech is in their first ever College Football Playoff appearance.

"If we don't act, we are very quickly going to be in a world of 30 to 50 college football teams that are basically a mini NFL."

A US Senator · White House college sports meeting · March 2026

That's the structural pattern. The SEC and Big Ten have TV contracts no one can match. A few outliers like Miami and Texas Tech compete on individual billionaire money. For everyone else, the Group of Six and most of the ACC and Big 12 are becoming feeder leagues.

Section 05 · The federal government had to step in

When the problem gets this big, eventually someone in Washington tries to fix it.

President Trump has signed two executive orders on college sports in nine months. The second one is the most aggressive federal intervention into college athletics in modern history. The lawyers say it's probably unconstitutional. It might still create market chaos before the courts ever weigh in.

01

July 24, 2025 · "Saving College Sports"

President Trump signs the first executive order. Tries to ban pay for play NIL schemes and require wealthy programs to maintain Olympic sports scholarships. Didn't really do anything. Industry stakeholders called it a signal more than a solution.

02

April 3, 2026 · "Urgent National Action"

President Trump signs the second, more aggressive order. Limits athletes to 5 seasons in a 5 year window. Caps transfers at 1 before graduation. Schools that violate could lose federal funding. Effective August 1, 2026.

03

The legal problem.

Multiple sports lawyers told ESPN they expect courts to rule the order unconstitutional and unenforceable. The 2021 Supreme Court ruling that started all of this made clear the NCAA isn't exempt from antitrust laws. Federal limits on athlete movement are exactly the kind of restraint the original case struck down.

04

The chaos is the point.

Even if the order doesn't hold up legally, it creates immediate market uncertainty. Athletes don't know if they'll be eligible if they transfer. Schools don't know if they'll lose federal funding. The system is now operating under federal uncertainty.

"The whole educational system is going to go out of business because of this."

President Donald Trump · Roundtable on college sports · March 2026

And Congress is on the clock. The order is meant to force lawmakers to act. After five years of proposed bills, neither the House nor the Senate has held a single full vote. The SCORE Act has been delayed twice since September 2025. Cruz and Cantwell are still negotiating in the Senate, stalled on whether athletes should be classified as employees.

The trap. The NCAA can't fix this because antitrust law prevents them from agreeing to limit athlete earnings. The federal government may not have constitutional authority either. Congress hasn't voted in five years. Every potential solution runs into the same wall the 2021 Supreme Court ruling built.
Section 06 · The one thing that actually got better

Athletes are staying in school. That's the real win.

I mentioned this up top. It's worth coming back to with the numbers, because it's the strongest argument for the system working as intended.

NBA early entry · 2021
363

College players who filed as early entry candidates the year NIL started.

NBA early entry · 2025
106

Less than one third of the pre NIL number. Lowest in a decade.

Top player NIL · JT Toppin
$2.8M

Big 12 Player of the Year at Texas Tech. NIL valuation alone beats four years of an NBA second round contract.

Donovan Dent · UCLA transfer
$2M

Possible second round NBA pick, but his college NIL deal pays more than the NBA contract would.

The math is now obvious. Unless you're a guaranteed first round pick, you'll make more in college through NIL than on an NBA rookie contract. So players are finishing their college careers, developing further, earning degrees, and banking serious money. That's a win for the players, for fans, and for the college game.

But here's the catch. The athletes staying are concentrated in football and basketball. Which means even more money has to flow into those sports. Which means even more pressure on the rest of the system. The bright spot makes the other problems worse.
The closer

The problem isn't NIL. It's the system around it.

Athletes deserve to be paid. They generate the revenue. NIL was right. That part is settled.

What isn't settled is everything else. Universities are cutting academic programs to fund revenue sharing. Olympic sports are getting eliminated. Boosters are walking away. High school recruits are getting locked out. Talent is concentrating in two conferences. The federal government has tried to intervene twice and probably can't legally do it. Congress hasn't voted in five years.

The free market created a system nobody can fix. Universities are choosing football and basketball over their academic mission AND their Olympic sports AND their athletes who aren't superstars. Even the federal government can't stop it. The benefits are real, but they come at a structural cost that's restructuring American higher education from the inside out.

The question isn't whether to pay college athletes. That's settled. The question is whether the system around them is sustainable, and right now it isn't.

What I found

Three things stood out.

One. The academic and athletic stories are the same story. You can't write about Syracuse cutting 93 programs without writing about the $20.5M per year revenue share schools owe their athletes starting July 2025. Same balance sheet.

Two. The federal government probably can't fix this. The 2021 Supreme Court ruling made the NCAA subject to antitrust law. Almost any restriction on athlete movement or pay can be challenged. Even if President Trump's order holds up, it doesn't solve the financial problem underneath.

Three. The benefits are real but narrow. Athletes staying in school is good. Stars finally being paid is fair. But those gains are concentrated in a small slice of athletes at a small slice of schools, and everyone else is paying for it.

The real question isn't whether to pay college athletes. It's whether the system around them is sustainable.

Gabriel Gomez · Miami · 2026

Sources & method

The data spine. All numbers in this piece are verified from public sources. The House v. NCAA settlement figures come from the approved settlement documents filed June 6, 2025. The Olympic sports cuts are tracked from coaching association statements and individual school announcements between May 2024 and April 2026. The retention percentages for the 2026 transfer portal cycle are reported by CBS Sports. The NBA Draft early entry numbers come from the official NCAA draft tracker. The executive order language is pulled directly from the White House published orders dated July 24, 2025 and April 3, 2026.

What this piece doesn't try to do. It doesn't take a side on whether athletes should be paid. They should be, and they are, and that's the fair part of all of this. It doesn't predict whether the second executive order will hold up in court. The lawyers are split. It doesn't argue that the SEC and Big Ten are villains. They're operating rationally in the market the 2021 Supreme Court ruling created. The piece is structural. It names what the system actually built, and what it's costing.
