Market Analysis · Consumer Behavior · No. 01

How GLP-1s are quietly rewriting $55 billion of America's grocery basket.

Nearly a quarter of US households already have someone on a GLP-1. Their grocery baskets are shrinking and shifting at the same time. JPMorgan estimates $30 to $55 billion in food and beverage sales could be wiped out by 2030. The brands that adapt fast will own the next decade. The ones that don't are already watching it show up in their earnings reports.

Section 01 · Consumer behavior shift

The grocery basket is shrinking and changing at the same time.

GLP-1 drugs like Ozempic, Wegovy, and Zepbound don't just suppress appetite. They change what people taste and crave. Less savory, less sweet, more protein, more fresh. The result is showing up in shopping data across the country, and the picture has gotten a lot clearer in the last few months.

Sugary snacks down about 10%. Ultra-processed foods taking the biggest hit. But there's a surprise hiding in the data: eggs and meat are also dropping in GLP-1 households.

Important caveat on the meat and eggs drop. Some of this is real GLP-1 behavior. But two other things are happening: egg prices spiked from avian flu, and GLP-1 users are shifting WITHIN protein. They're eating less fatty red meat and processed meat, while eating MORE chicken, fish, and dairy protein. So "meat" isn't one trend. Lean protein is actually winning. Fatty and processed meat is losing.

What's clearly going up isn't ambiguous though. Yogurt is the biggest single before-and-after gainer in GLP-1 households. Fresh produce purchases climb after about a year on the medication. Drinkable protein and functional beverages are outperforming. The "less but better" basket is becoming the new American grocery cart.

Section 02 · Category contraction · Who's losing

The snack aisle is taking the first hit. And it's already in the filings.

This isn't a forecast anymore. The decline is showing up in real public company earnings reports right now. Investors are watching it. CPG strategy teams are scrambling.

01

General Mills · U.S. Snacks.

Q3 FY26 net sales down high-single digits for U.S. Snacks. North America Retail segment down 14% overall. The company is repositioning leadership to address it.

Source · General Mills 8-K, March 2026

02

Ultra-processed snack brands.

Calorie-dense snacks down around 10% in GLP-1 households. Chips, cookies, candy, sugary cereals taking the biggest hits. The "treat yourself" purchase is becoming the first cut.

Source · Journal of Marketing Research, 2025

03

Fatty & processed meat (lean meat is winning).

Red meat, hot dogs, and sausage are dropping in GLP-1 households. Egg prices spiked from avian flu, not GLP-1. Meanwhile lean protein like chicken, fish, and dairy is gaining share. The shift is happening WITHIN protein, not against it.

Source · Cornell SC Johnson + USDA inflation data, 2025-2026

04

Sugary beverages.

GLP-1 changes how sweet foods taste. Many users report sodas and sugary drinks become unpleasant. The traditional soft drink market is now structurally exposed.

Source · Tate & Lyle research, 2026

JPMorgan's projection: $30 to $55 billion in annual food and beverage sales could be wiped out by 2030. That's roughly the size of the entire US breakfast cereal market. Disappearing.
Section 03 · Category growth · Who's winning

The brands repositioning now will own the next decade.

Some companies are moving fast. Nestlé just launched its first new brand in 30 years specifically for GLP-1 users. General Mills, Danone, and Coca-Cola are reformulating across portfolios. The winners are already separating from the rest.

01

Nestlé · Vital Pursuit.

Launched in 2024. First new Nestlé brand in nearly 30 years. Nutrient-dense ready-to-eat meals targeted at GLP-1 users. High protein, portion controlled, satiety focused.

Source · FoodNavigator, January 2026

02

Yogurt brands.

Yogurt is the biggest single before-and-after gainer in GLP-1 households. High protein, low sugar, satiating, portion controlled. Brands like Chobani and Oikos are positioned perfectly.

Source · Gizmodo / Journal of Marketing Research, 2025

03

Drinkable protein and functional beverages.

Shakes and smoothies outperforming traditional snacks. GLP-1 users drink fewer fluids overall, so each beverage has to earn its calories. Functional positioning is winning.

Source · Tastewise GLP-1 CPG Playbook, April 2026

04

Big CPG players adapting fast.

Danone emphasizing high-protein, low-sugar across European and US lines. Coca-Cola growing high-protein and low-sugar ranges. General Mills reorganizing leadership to address the snack decline.

Source · CBC News, IFT, McGill Journal of Economics, 2026

By 2030, GLP-1 households will represent 35% of all food and beverage units sold in the United States. That number alone reshapes every CPG strategy. The question isn't whether to reposition. It's how fast.

Putting real dollar figures on what's at stake.

A quick way to read this. The losing categories add up to roughly $190 billion in US sales. Not all of it disappears. But even a small percentage shift across $190 billion in sales is real money. The winning categories are smaller but growing faster, which is exactly what you'd expect when consumers shift habits. The total food and beverage industry is so big ($2.63 trillion) that the GLP-1 shift looks tiny in percentage terms but huge in absolute dollars.

Section 05 · Who's ahead, who's behind

Two questions decide who wins. How much could you lose, and how fast did you react?

Every major food and beverage company sits somewhere on this map. Companies whose products are heavily affected by the GLP-1 shift are on the right side. Companies that have already responded with new products or strategy moves are on the top. The most interesting box is the bottom-right: high impact, no public response yet. That's where investors are watching.

The bottom-right box is the one that matters. Mondelez, Hershey, and Kraft Heinz sell exactly the products GLP-1 users are cutting back on. They haven't announced major repositioning yet. That doesn't mean they're doing nothing internally. It does mean the public hasn't seen a plan, which leaves investors and retailers guessing. PepsiCo is in a unique spot: its drinks business is moving, but its Frito-Lay snacks unit is the biggest US salty snack player and the most exposed of all.

How I got these numbers Cornell University's December 2025 study found that GLP-1 households cut their food spending by about 5.3% within 6 months. I applied that drop to three adoption rates from Circana's research firm. The math: number of US adults on GLP-1 × $9,660 average annual food spending per adult × 5.3% spending reduction. The $9,660 figure comes from the USDA Economic Research Service, which reports total US food spending of $2.58 trillion in 2024. Divided across 267 million US adults (US Census, Vintage 2024), that's $9,660 per adult per year on food. This includes both groceries and away-from-home dining, because GLP-1 users cut both. The model's middle case ($30B) sits inside JPMorgan's published $30-55B range for 2030 industry impact. Either way, the direction is the same. Big shift, real dollars, accelerating fast.

Even the slowest scenario costs the food and beverage industry $20 billion a year. That's roughly the size of the entire US yogurt market projected for 2030. The middle scenario approaches the annual revenue of Kraft Heinz. The fast scenario equals more than the entire US salty snack category. Whichever way you slice it, this is real money moving.

The closer · Market trajectory

America just changed how it eats. The companies that adapt will own the next decade.

The trajectory only points one way. Gallup's Q3 2025 survey shows 12.4% of US adults now on injectable GLP-1s, more than double the 2024 number. Circana counts 23% of US households with at least one user, and 2.9 million new prescriptions added between September 2024 and September 2025. Circana projects adoption hitting 21% of adults by 2034.

And the next wave is just starting. Oral GLP-1 formulations are launching in the US in 2026 and Europe in 2027, which could drop prices by up to 50%. Insurance coverage is expanding. New competitors are entering the market. Meanwhile the brands at the top of the snack and sugary beverage categories are watching real declines hit their earnings reports right now.

Every CPG brand right now has one question to answer. How does my product fit a GLP-1 grocery basket? The companies with a confident answer are the ones writing new strategy decks. The ones still figuring it out are watching their categories shrink in real time.

The American grocery basket has been reshaped a few times before. Low-fat in the 80s. Low-carb in the early 2000s. Organic in the 2010s. None of those came with a pharmaceutical mechanism behind them, and none of them moved this fast.

What I found

Three things stood out.

One. The losers are already showing up in the filings. This isn't a forecast you can debate. General Mills' Q3 FY26 8-K showed U.S. snacks down high-single digits and the North America Retail segment down 14%. Real public companies are taking the hit right now.

Two. The story is more nuanced than headlines suggest. Eggs dropped because of avian flu, not GLP-1. Within protein, GLP-1 users are eating LESS fatty red meat and processed meat but MORE chicken, fish, and dairy. The categories cleanly dropping (sugary snacks, sugary drinks, ultra-processed foods) are where the GLP-1 shift shows up unfiltered.

Three. The winners are the brands that moved early. Nestlé launched Vital Pursuit in 2024. Yogurt brands repositioned. Drinkable protein scaled fast. Speed of response is the differentiator. The next 18 months will decide which CPG brands own the GLP-1 era.

The bigger takeaway isn't really about Ozempic. It's about how fast a structural shift can travel through a $1 trillion industry once the data starts compounding. By the time the trend hits the headlines, the strategy window is already closing.

Sources & method

The data spine. Every number in this piece is sourced from a publicly available report, peer-reviewed study, or SEC filing. The adoption figures come from Gallup's Q2-Q3 2025 survey and Circana's November 2025 research. Spending data comes from the Cornell SC Johnson study published in the Journal of Marketing Research in December 2025. Caloric reduction data comes from Technomic's Q2 2025 report. Industry forecasts come from JPMorgan and Circana. Brand response data was sourced from Nestlé's own product announcements, General Mills public earnings releases (8-K filings), and reporting by FoodNavigator, CNBC, and the McGill Journal of Economics. Inflation context on meat and eggs comes from USDA price index data.

What this piece doesn't try to do. It doesn't take a position on GLP-1 drugs as healthcare interventions. That's a separate debate. It doesn't predict which individual brands will win. It identifies categories and patterns. It doesn't claim CPG companies are doing anything wrong. They're operating in a market that's shifting faster than most strategy cycles. The piece is structural. It names what's happening and what it means for consumer goods strategy.
